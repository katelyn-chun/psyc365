Is That Bear Real?
Or Did You Imagine It?

Your brain sees a bear. But did you actually see one or did your imagination conjure it? New research reveals the neural mechanism that keeps your inner world and outer reality apart.

Dijkstra, von Rein, Kok & Fleming (2025)
"A neural basis for distinguishing imagination from reality"
Neuron, Vol. 113, Issue 15 · DOI: 10.1016/j.neuron.2025.05.015

Overview

Your brain is a remarkably bad liar detector…for itself.

🧸

The Rationale

When you imagine something vividly like a bear, an apple, or a face many of the same brain regions fire as when you actually perceive it. Turns out the brain reuses its own perceptual machinery for mental imagery. It's efficient, but it creates a problem: If imagining a bear activates similar visual cortex regions as seeing a real bear, why don't we constantly mistake imaginations for reality?

🫧

The Knowledge Gap

Previous research has shown that imagination and perception overlap in the brain. But the specific neural mechanisms behind computing reality judgements moment by moment that keeps reality and fantasy sorted apart was unknown. That was the gap this study set out to fill.

💡

The Questions

Big picture question: How does the brain tell whether a visual experience comes from the outside world or from imagination?

Research question: Which brain regions and neural signals predict whether people judge an experience as real versus imagined, especially when imagery is vivid enough to cause confusion?

💭

The Hypothesis

Hypothesis: The hypothesis is that reality judgements depend on the strength of sensory activity in mid-level visual cortex, especially the fusiform gyrus, and this activity is integrated with a frontal network that makes the final reality decision.

🐾 · 🐾 · 🐾
The Experiment

Study Design

Basic Setup

26 participants are put in an fMRI scanner. They were shown a screen with visual "noise", which looked like TV static.

Task

They were asked two things at once: detecting whether a grating was actually appearing in the noise, and imagine a grating in their head. The stripes were made so faint that participants could only detect them correctly about 70% of the time.

Congruent vs. Incongruent

Participants were split into two conditions per block: Congruent (imagine and look for a left-leaning stripe) and Incongruent (imagine a left-leaning stripe while looking for a right-leaning one)

Participants judged: "Was something really there?

On each trial, they made a reality judgment and rated the vividness of their mental imagery.

The Reality Threshold Model

The researchers tested the Reality threshold model (M₁), where imaginary and perception signals are added together into a single "reality signal" with a threshold to determines whether something is judged as real, versus a Null model (M₀), where imaginary and perception signals are driven by separate, independent signals.

Variables

Independent + Dependent Variables

Independent

  • Stimulus presence: if a stimulus was actually shown to the participant.
    • The variable was manipulated through the separation of either having, or not having a grating present.
  • Imagery congruency: how the participants were instructed to imagine the orientation of the grating versus the actual orientation they were trying to detect.
    • Manipulated in a blockwise fashion with two conditions; the congruent and incongruent condition.

Dependent

  • Detection responses: the participant's decision on whether a stimulus was real.
    • Measured by the participant's judgement indicating whether they believed a grating had really been presented on screen.
  • Vividness rating of the imagery: clarity of participant's internal mental image.
    • Measured by the participants' rating of vividness of their mental imagery after the reality judgement on every trial.
  • The fMRI BOLD signal:
    • Used to track activity fluctuations and BOLD signals in regions of interest and analyze the signals to find out how they tracked the reality signal and predicted behavioural confusions.
Results

When imagination goes vivid, reality gets blurry.

Behavioural Results

Imagination reliably bled into reality judgments and felt more vivid when the congruent stimulus was present.

37% false alarm rate for congruent

Participants reported seeing a grating that wasn't there when their imagination matched what they were looking for.

37% false alarm rate for incongruent

Fewer false alarms when imagining a different orientation.

Imagery felt more vivid when real stimulus present

Vividness was significantly boosted by the real stimulus in the congruent condition (p = 0.00002), but not the incongruent (BF = 0.89).

Computational Model

M₀ is rejected over M₁, as M₁ provided a better fit. The brain combines imagery and perception additively, which means there is no clean firewall between what you imagine and what you see.

Neural System

The fusiform gyrus tracks how strong the combined signal is, and the anterior insula makes reality judgement base on this. Early visual cortex and the intraparietal sulcus were both ruled out as reality monitors. EVC suppresses irrelevant signals, and IPS suppresses external inputs during stimulus-present trials.

💬 Why We Should Care

Author Interpretations + Implications

Interpretations 🐼

The authors of the study interpreted the results as evidence for a two-stage neural mechanism that allows the brain to distinguish between imagination and reality. They emphasize that the reality signal is summed together, rather than maintained separately, in the fusiform gyrus by the perception and imagination channels of the brain.

Additionally, the authors state that when the combined strength of the reality signal crosses a certain (internal) threshold, the brain infers the signal reflects external reality, supporting the "reality threshold" model. They also interpret the anterior insula to "read out" sensory evidence to make a decision for the real/not real judgment.

Finally, their results lead to the idea that confusions between imagination and reality occur when sensory activity created by an internal image is strong enough to mirror the activity usually produced by a real stimulus.

Implications 🐻‍❄️

For researchers:
Challenging the "Tag" Theory: some theories suggest the brain "tags" self-generated signals for distinguishing imagination, but the authors' results suggest that control signals associated with self-generated perception are not enough to distinguish imagination from reality.
Reality Monitoring: their findings showed that the reality signal was supported by activity in higher- rather than lower-level visual regions. Perceptual reality monitoring could actually function more effectively at the level of objects rather than low-level stimulus features, as objects are central to both navigation and (internal) simulation and planning. This opens up the possibility for future research to further investigate the brain's organization of simulations and navigation.

For everyone:
The study helps explain how we normally tell what is real, and why we may believe that we see something generated merely from our own thoughts.

Critique

Strengths
  • - Robust within-subject design
  • - Computational model validated against real data
  • - Trial-by-trial prediction of confusion errors
  • - Rigorous fMRI cluster correction (FWE p<0.05)
Limitations
  • - Small sample size (n = 26)
  • - Simple gratings only, not complex imagery
  • - Only visual modality tested
  • - Correlational causality not established
🐻 Take-home message

Your brain doesn't have separate boxes for "real" and "imagined." It has a single meter the fusiform gyrus that combines both signals and calls anything above a threshold real. Vivid imagination doesn't just feel real. To your brain, for a moment, it is.
